Why current matching becomes much harder in triple-junction solar cells

One of the main reasons triple-junction solar cells are difficult is that the current generated by each sub-cell must be balanced very carefully. In a monolithic device, the sub-cells are connected in series, so the overall current is limited by the weakest current-producing junction. That makes the design space much narrower than in a single-junction device, and narrower still than in a two-junction tandem.

In practice, this means that every layer above the lower sub-cells matters. The top transparent electrode, recombination layer, transport layers, and even the optical thickness of intermediate films all affect how much light reaches each absorber. A small parasitic loss in the front of the stack does not just reduce the top cell current. It also reduces the photon flux available to the middle and bottom cells.

This is why transparent conducting electrodes and interconnection layers become so important. They must carry charge efficiently, remain optically transparent, and avoid introducing additional recombination or reflection losses. If the resistivity is too high, fill factor suffers. If the optical loss is too large, current matching is lost. The challenge is therefore not simply to make a good electrode, but to make an electrode that is good enough for the entire stack.

In triple-junction devices, the optimisation problem becomes highly coupled. Improving one layer may help one sub-cell while hurting another. That is why modelling, optical design, and interface engineering have to be considered together rather than separately. In my view, that coupling is exactly what makes triple-junction photovoltaics scientifically interesting.
